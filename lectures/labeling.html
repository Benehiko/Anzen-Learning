Where are we now?

We have now looked at the first three elements of information architecture. We will be looking at the fourth, Labelling.

What is Labelling?

Labelling is used to give names to information elements, as well as the links between these elements. As mentioned earlier (in the quiz), as link sentence such as "Please click here for more information" is much easier to read than "Please visit www.myaswesomewebsite.com/files/images/productimages/section33429/imag443.jpg for more information".

How do we do it?

Numerous ways for labelling information are available. Some examples include:

  • Use Analogies or metaphors. This has been used in computers for a long time. Think of "recycle bin", "folders", "sandbox" and "shop".
  • Use graphics or text. Icons can be used for labelling information. Expecting users to click on a picture or one word may be more intuitive than providing all the text of the link.

The issue with labels

Have a look at the following use of labels:

"Please visit our Wagabaloo area."

"Click here for the pantry" (on a site unrelated to food)

"Kill a mockingbird (a link on a site)

These are examples of labels that may confuse a user. It is not self-explanatory and does not contribute to an understanding of the nature of the information (or links) that are being labelled.

And this is the problem we have with labels. It needs to be short, but also self-explanatory. It should be creative but not ambiguous.
